# Assignment 3 — Crazy 8s and Competitor Analysis
### Institutional Knowledge Doesn't Scale — AUI UX Bootcamp

---

## Part A: Crazy 8s

Eight different directions, sketched fast, on purpose kept different from each other rather than eight versions of the same idea.

### 1. Tip Drop
Right after a student solves something (an office visit, a registration fix, a club form), a one-tap prompt appears: "Got that sorted? Leave a 30-second tip for the next student." The tip gets tagged to a situation (visa, funding, Add/Drop) automatically.

### 2. Ask Without a Face
A question board scoped to specific situations, not a general feed. You post under a topic like "housing" or "registration," not your name, so asking never signals that you're "supposed to already know."

### 3. Mentor Handoff Kit
A short, guided form that every outgoing RA, club officer, TA, or mentor fills out in about ten minutes before they leave the role. It becomes visible to whoever takes over next, instead of living in one person's head or personal Drive.

### 4. Verified by Time
Every tip shows when it was last confirmed as "still true" by another student. Old advice visibly fades instead of quietly misleading someone the way the scholarship rumor did.

### 5. The Handbook's Practical Twin
A layer added right next to the existing AUI portal and handbook pages. Next to each official policy, a short community note on what actually happens in practice.

### 6. Timed Nudges
A notification tied to the academic calendar that surfaces the exact tip a student needs, right when it matters: "Internship season really starts in October, not spring." "Club funding requests open this week."

### 7. Matched to a Real Senior
Instead of a public feed, a lightweight matching tool connects a question to one or two students who've opted in as knowledgeable about that exact situation, based on major, club, or experience (exchange, transfer, international).

### 8. The Rites-of-Passage Map
A browsable map of things every student eventually learns, organized by student type (freshman, transfer, exchange, international) so a newcomer can browse by their own situation instead of needing to already know what to search for.

## Part B: Competitor Analysis

What students already use today, and where each one falls short against what our interviews actually showed mattered: capturing real practical knowledge, low social cost to ask, AUI-specific, surviving past graduation, and being verified.

| Solution | Captures practical knowledge | Low social cost to ask | AUI-specific | Survives graduating classes | Verified / trustworthy |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| AUI handbook, portal, LMS | No — policy only | N/A | Yes | Yes, but content goes stale on practice | High for policy, but interviews show it often doesn't match real office behavior |
| Mentor / RA / TA / advisor programs | Yes, this is where it actually lives | No — asking a real person carries status pressure | Yes | No — leaves when that person graduates | High, but one person's experience can go outdated |
| WhatsApp / Facebook groups, confession pages | Yes | Partial — safer than asking one person, still not anonymous | Yes (class or exchange groups) | Partial — history exists but is buried in scroll, membership resets yearly | No — this is exactly where the false scholarship rumor spread |
| Anonymous campus apps (Fizz, Sidechat, Yik Yak) | Partial — mixed with general social content | Yes, full anonymity | No — general consumer apps, not scoped to AUI or to practical questions | No — feed-based, content scrolls away | No — public reporting has repeatedly flagged harassment and moderation problems on these apps, and some campuses have restricted or banned them for it |
| Reddit / Quora / Substack | Partial — good for generic advice | Yes | No — no AUI-specific community with real numbers | Technically archived, but not school-specific or current | Mixed — upvotes are a weak proxy for accuracy |
| General AI assistants | No — doesn't know AUI's undocumented local practice | Yes | No | N/A | Can sound confident while being wrong about local specifics |
| Course Q&A tools (Piazza, Ed Discussion style) | Partial — course content only | Partial — usually tied to your real name in class | Only within a course, not campus-wide | No — resets every semester | High within scope, moderated by the instructor or TA |

**What this shows:** nothing on this list is both AUI-specific and built to survive past a single cohort. The peer channels (mentors, group chats) are the only ones that actually hold the right kind of knowledge, but they're the least durable. The most durable channels (handbook, LMS) hold the least useful kind of knowledge. That gap, AUI-specific, situation-scoped, low social cost, and durable across graduating classes, is where an opportunity sits, and it's what Crazy 8s concepts 1, 3, 4, and 6 above are aimed at directly.
